From Forecasts to Decisions: Visualization for Trust and Transparency in Public Health in the Era of AI

Research Objective System Features Information
  • ● Sparse data weakens confidence and makes outputs harder to validate. Infectious disease forecasts rely on surveillance data streams such as case reports, hospitalizations, and wastewater signals. Sparse or noisy reporting weakens confidence in model outputs and complicates validation.

  • ● Data quality and signal detection. Decision-makers frequently confront uncertainty about whether observed anomalies represent real outbreaks or artifacts from inconsistent reporting, missing data, or delayed submissions. Without clear indications of which inputs are influencing the forecast, users risk overreacting to spurious patterns or underreacting to genuine threats.

  • ● Understanding model mechanics and decision justification. Beyond distinguishing signal from noise, public health officials need insight into how forecasts are generated. Black-box models obscure the relationships between inputs (e.g., test positivity, mobility, hospitalizations) and predicted trends, making it difficult to justify actions, communicate risk, or optimize interventions.

  • ● Uncertainty communication. Forecasts inevitably carry uncertainty, but if confidence intervals are overly wide, they become operationally unusable. For example, during COVID-19, state health departments received hospitalization surge forecasts with wide uncertainty ranges, sometimes spanning hundreds of admissions. Even though the models were scientifically valid, administrators could not translate those ranges into concrete staffing or supply decisions.
